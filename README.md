The Smart Biomass Reduction System
I started this project because of a smell. Every summer, the bin under our sink would fill with wet food scraps — watermelon rinds, vegetable peels — and within a day it would start leaking a dark, sour liquid. I later learned this liquid has a name: leachate. It is one of the main sources of groundwater contamination near landfills, and it starts exactly where I was standing, in a kitchen.
Recycling and not letting trash pile up are things almost anyone can manage — small, low-effort habits we already do every day. But drying fruit is different, especially for Iranians, who eat fruit constantly, every single day. Doing it by hand is slow and tedious enough that most people quit after a few tries, simply out of laziness. I know because I did the same thing — it really is that tiring.
So I built a drying system that takes the manual part out completely. The device sits between you and your trash bin: you drop your scraps into the top compartment, they pass through the dryer, and the dried waste falls into a bin underneath, ready to be emptied. No patience required, nothing to babysit — you just toss your scraps in and go watch a movie.
I wanted to build something that stopped that process before it began, not after.
The machine I designed, the Smart Biomass Reduction System, is a 30-centimeter horizontal unit that takes wet organic waste and turns it into a dry, odorless powder in about five minutes, cutting its volume by roughly 80%. It works in four stages on a single shaft: a chopping section with offset blades that grabs waste without letting it spin uselessly in place, a screw press that crushes it hard enough to rupture plant cell walls and force out water, a decanter centrifuge that spins the pulp at high speed to separate the remaining liquid, and a hot-air drying tunnel that finishes the job using a ceramic heating element instead of direct metal contact.
None of these four stages were the hard part on their own. The hard part was making them work together inside one shaft without one stage destroying another.
The first real failure was soft waste — banana peels, wet leaves — just spinning in circles at the intake instead of moving forward. I fixed it by offsetting the opening and adding fixed ridges inside the wall, so the rotation itself pins the waste against the ridges and forces it down instead of letting it spin.
The second failure showed up in the centrifuge. Crushed fruit skin is sticky, and it clogged the fine filter slots almost immediately. My first instinct was a scraper blade, but plastic wore out in weeks and added drag that strained the motor. The fix that actually worked was to stop scraping altogether: I ran the inner screw and the outer perforated cylinder in the same direction but at different speeds, so the speed difference itself continuously wipes the screen clean, with no contact and nothing to wear down.
The third failure was mechanical. Pressing wet waste with enough force to break it open also bends a thin shaft like a bow, and a bent shaft ruins the 0.3mm tolerances the centrifuge depends on. I solved this by anchoring the shaft from both ends instead of one — a high-torque motor on one side, a heat-resistant bearing on the other — which kept deflection under 0.1mm.
The last failure was heat traveling backward through the metal shaft from the 120°C drying zone, slowly cooking the centrifuge seals and the motor grease. I separated the two zones with a ceramic thermal-barrier coupling, so rotation passes through but heat doesn't, and I moved the heat itself into airflow rather than into the metal.
What I ended up with matters less to me than how I got there. Each fix came from watching the machine fail in a specific, physical way and asking what was actually happening at that exact point, not from a general idea of what "should" work. If the waste this system processes never becomes leachate, never becomes methane, never needs a truck to haul it away — that's the outcome I was aiming for. But the part of this project I keep coming back to is the habit it built in me: to look at a failure closely enough that the fix becomes obvious.
